I am a postdoctoral appointee at the Donostia International Physics Center (DIPC). My research revolves around a broad range of topics in Cosmology and Astrophysics, including the galaxy-halo connection, the cross-correlation of large scale structure and cosmic microwave background, estimation of photometric redshifts, and characterization of active galactic nuclei.

My principal lines of work are:

  1. Missing baryons. A complete census of baryons in the late universe is challenging due to the intermediate temperate and rarefied character of intergalactic gas. In my work, I leverage the cross-correlation of angular redshift fluctuations, a novel observable presenting a strong correlation with the cosmic density and velocity fields, and cosmic microwave background observations to set constraints on the abundance, location, and properties of intergalactic gas.
  2. Forward-modelling galaxy colors. The next generation of large-scale galaxy surveys will observe hundreds of millions of galaxies with both unprecedented precision. Fully exploiting these surveys will require a precise control of systematics and pushing cosmological analysis towards highly non-linear scales. To do so, I am working on forward-modelling galaxy observations, i.e. producing synthetic galaxy catalogs presenting the same properties as actual galaxies from observations.
  3. Quasars. Quasars are among the brightest objects in the cosmos, and thus these cosmic beasts enable accessing the distance universe. My work revolves around detecting these sources in narrow-band surveys and estimating their properties.
  4. Assembly bias. A long-standing prediction of structure formation theories is that the clustering of dark matter halos only depends upon their mass. However, cosmological simulations show that the clustering of halos depends upon their concentration, spin, formation time, etc. This effect is commonly known as halo assembly bias and it has been shown to also affect galaxy clustering. I am working on studying the origin of this effect and its influence on the large-scale distribution of galaxies.
