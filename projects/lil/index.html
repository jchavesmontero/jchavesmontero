The lensing-is-low problem

It is now widely recognized that standard models describing the connection between galaxies and dark matter halos overestimate the gravitational lensing of massive galaxies when optimized to reproduce their clustering. This issue is commonly referred to as the "lensing-is-low problem." One prevalent interpretation of this problem is that it represents another manifestation of the tension between measurements of structure growth in the early and late Universe, known as the S8 tension. However, in Chaves-Montero, Angulo, and Contreras (2023) we showed that this problem reflects shortcomings in standard galaxy-halo connection models rather than tensions within the LCDM paradigm itself. Specifically, the problem stems from neglecting various effects related to galaxy formation, such as assembly bias, the spatial segregation of satellite galaxies in comparison to dark matter, and the influence of baryonic effects on the distribution of mass. All these effects contribute towards overestimating gravitational lensing, and when combined, explain the amplitude and scale dependence of the lensing-is-low problem. Consequently, we concluded that simplistic galaxy-halo connection models are inadequate for the simultaneous interpretation of clustering and lensing.

Having identified the root cause of the problem, our focus shifted towards finding a solution. In Contreras et al. (2023), we attempted to jointly model the clustering of galaxies from a state-of-the-art hydrodynamical simulation using the SubHalo Abundance Matching extended model (SHAMe), a very advanced galaxy-halo connection model. We found that SHAMe successfully reproduces the projected clustering, redshift-space correlation function multipoles, and excess surface density of simulated galaxies within statistic uncertainties down to 100 kiloparsecs. Therefore, this model does not suffer from the "artificial" lensing-is-low problem.

Encouraged by these results, in Contreras, Chaves-Montero, and Angulo (2023), we employed SHAMe to study the clustering and lensing properties of galaxies observed in the BOSS survey. Our study revealed that SHAMe accurately reproduces the clustering and lensing of BOSS galaxies at all redshifts, confirming that SHAMe does not suffer from the lensing-is-low problem. In addition, this finding opens the possibility of using this model to constraint cosmology jointly using clustering and lensing on small scales. Subsequently, we sought to replicate the clustering and lensing of BOSS galaxies by evaluating SHAMe in a simulation using a low S8 cosmology. Our analysis demonstrated that SHAMe also reproduces these observables for this particular cosmology, indicating that the precision of clustering and lensing measurements obtained from BOSS galaxies is insufficient for distinguishing between the Planck and low S8 cosmologies. This degeneracy arises due to the flexible nature of the galaxy-halo connection in SHAMe, which enables solving the lensing-is-low problem but compromises its cosmological constraining power. Conversely, SHAMe makes distinct predictions regarding the galaxy-halo connection for these two cosmologies, such as varying levels of galaxy assembly bias.

Presently, our ongoing research involves modeling additional observables that constraint galaxy assembly bias using SHAME, which will enable enhancing the constraining power of the method and enable us to establish precise constraints on both cosmology and the galaxy-halo connection.
